Everything we tested, in plain English

Six rounds of work on one question: does spatial positional encoding help predict survival? Here is what we tried, why, what happened, and what to do now.

The whole story in five lines
  • The original pipeline's positional encoding was broken maths — it was secretly just counting cells.
  • Fixing the data handling (not the encoding) took the score from 0.635 → 0.776.
  • We replaced the encoding with a better one (RWSE). It failed too — and we found out why.
  • The reason is a proof, not bad luck: in a flat tissue map, "how connected is a cell" and "how crowded is the tissue" are the same number.
  • A new design finally escaped that trap (density link dropped from 0.82 → 0.13). It's the live lead.

01 First: what score are we even talking about?

Everything is measured with the C-index. Take any two patients. Did the model correctly say which one dies first? The C-index is how often it gets that right.

1.00 perfect 0.78 our best model today 0.55 ← what you get from SHUFFLED (fake) labels on this data 0.50 a coin flip
Trap #1 we walked into

Random guessing here scores 0.55, not 0.50. We measured this by shuffling the survival labels and re-running. So a model scoring 0.536 — like the original pipeline's encoding — is not "slightly better than chance." It is worse than chance. The old report treated 0.5 as the floor and read that number as a small win.

02 Round 1 — the audit: the encoding was broken

Why we looked: you asked what was reducing performance.

The pipeline built a graph of cells and took its Laplacian Eigenmaps (LE) — 8 numbers per cell, pooled into a mean block and a std block per sample. We checked what those 16 numbers actually contained:

The 8 "std" features, every sample: std of eigenvector k = 1/√N ← N = number of cells. That's it. Proof (2 lines of maths, verified in code): eigenvectors are orthonormal → each has length 1 → std = √(1/N − mean²) they're orthogonal to the trivial one → mean ≈ 0 → std ≈ 1/√N So we tested it: the 8 PE "std" features alone .... C = 0.5491 just the cell count, one number ... C = 0.5495 ← identical

Eight "spatial" features were eight copies of one number: how many cells are in the image. The 8 "mean" features were near-zero rounding noise.

And the comparison it was benchmarked against was also broken

"Raw Coordinates" was supposed to be the honest baseline. It pooled the X/Y coordinates — but the coordinates had already been z-scored per sample, which forces every sample's mean to 0 and std to 1. So:

variance of "Raw Coordinates" across all 308 samples: 8.6e-17 ← that is zero. Every sample had the identical feature vector.
The headline claim was two encodings of cell count

The original report's win — "PE beats raw coordinates, 0.536 vs 0.526" — compared cell-count-in-disguise against a constant. Both below the 0.55 chance line. There was no result there.

03 Round 2 — your idea: weight the graph with biology

Why: you asked whether the graph could carry marker/biology info instead of just X-Y geometry. Good instinct — pure geometry throws away 39 markers of information.

What we built: edges weighted by both closeness in space and similarity in markers:

wij = exp(−distance² / 2σs²) × exp(−marker difference² / 2σf²) └── close in space ──┘ └── similar biology ──┘

And we swapped LE for RWSE (random-walk structural encoding), because LE has a known flaw: its eigenvectors can flip sign or rotate freely, so sample A's "feature 3" isn't sample B's "feature 3" — they aren't comparable. RWSE has no sign and no basis. It asks: release a random walker at this cell; what's the chance it's back here after t steps? That is well-defined on every graph.

Result: it lost. All three times.

Two things to notice. Marker weighting made it worse every single time — your specific idea, tested honestly, lost. And a 5-number density control (cell count, density, degree, etc.) beat every clever representation we built.

Why it lost — the number that explains everything

Can we predict RWSE just from density? (R² = 1.0 means "perfectly") RWSE on plain graph ......... R² = 0.72 (12 of 16 dims above 0.5) RWSE on marker-weighted ..... R² = 0.82 (16 of 16 dims above 0.5) ← WORSE

RWSE was 72–82% just density wearing a costume. And weighting made it more density-bound, not less — because the marker kernel traps the walker inside pockets of similar cells, and pocket size is... density again.

04 Round 3 — why it was doomed from the start

Why we looked: you sent a paper on building weighted adjacency matrices and asked for valid mechanisms. Reading it sent us back to the graph itself.

Trap #2: k_neighbors = 10 is not a constant

A fixed k is a fixed rank, not a fixed distance. In a crowded sample the 10th-nearest neighbour is close; in a sparse one it's far away. Measured on your data:

the 10th neighbour sits at: 12.05 µm → 38.17 µm (3.17× spread) correlation with density: −0.835

So the graph silently asks a different physical question of every sample, and density decides which one. It's the per-sample z-scoring bug again — hiding one level down, in a line that looks like a constant.

Worse: your graph reaches ~18 µm. NCEM measured where real cell-to-cell signalling happens across 5 platforms — 69 ± 14 µm. The graph is about 4× too small to see the biology.

The proof: connectivity and crowding are the same thing

Correlation of degree with density: kNN −0.874 · radius +0.927.

Look at the two extremes together and the trap is obvious:

  • Fix k → every cell has ~10 neighbours by construction. Degree barely moves (11.19 ± 0.23). The tiny bit that does move correlates with density at −0.874. The walker has nothing left to measure except density.
  • Use a radius → degree is free to move, but now degree = density × π r². The walker is measuring density directly, by definition.
The general result — this is a real finding, worth the thesis

In a flat (2-D) map of points, degree and density are the same variable. You can pin it and have nothing to measure, or free it and measure density. There is no third option reachable by changing the geometry — no value of k, σ, or walk length escapes it.

So RWSE's R² of 0.82 wasn't bad luck or a tuning failure. It was the only thing that could have happened. The escape isn't a better geometric graph — it's putting biology on the edges so the thing you measure stops being a function of point spacing.

05 Round 4 — the design that finally escaped

Why: the paper you sent (STMGAMF) turned out to argue against what we'd built. It does not multiply space × biology into one weight. It keeps two separate graphs and combines them later.

what we built (lost): one graph, weight = space × biology → either term can veto an edge; biology carves density pockets what the paper does: two graphs, A_spatial and A_marker, kept apart → geometry says WHO is adjacent; biology says WHAT they are

We couldn't use their model — it's a GCN, and with 27 events you cannot train one. But you don't need the network, only the message-passing step. UTAG showed a GCN layer with the learned weights deleted is just one line:

affinity = normalize(A, norm="l1") # each cell's neighbours sum to 1 X = affinity @ X # each cell averages its neighbours. That's it.

No training. Nothing to overfit. Repeat it 3× on each graph = a 3-layer GCN with the risky part removed.

I told you four times that nothing adds to the 0.776 baseline, because every block I added made it worse. Four out of four looked suspicious, so I ran the control I should have run first: add 12 columns of pure random noise.

Bars scaled 0.30–0.85. Adding literal random numbers costs −0.041. Every "penalty" I reported was that same number. RWSE's −0.033 was actually better than noise.

What went wrong, and why it matters

The forest picks √272 ≈ 16 features at random at each split. Add 12 new columns to 272 and they're offered ~6% of the time — they almost never get chosen, they just dilute. Permutation importance confirms it: shuffling the added columns changes the score by −0.005 ± 0.012. The forest never even looks at them.

So the "+X makes it worse" test was measuring dilution, not usefulness. It never had the power to detect a good block, so it never had the power to condemn one. The RWSE verdict still stands — that rests on R² 0.82 and on RWSE-alone scoring 0.514 inside the chance band, both independent of this test. But one of its three legs was rotten, and I should have caught it before reporting it.

07 The real ceiling: 27 events

This is the constraint behind everything above, and it deserves a plain statement.

The baseline the whole project is benchmarked against: 272 features ← 16 celltype proportions + 256 neighbour-matrix cells (16×16) 27 events ← patients who actually died of cancer events per variable = 27 / 272 = 0.10 the accepted rule of thumb for survival models is ≥ 10

You are 100× below the standard guideline. The model has 10 knobs for every scrap of evidence. This is why adding anything dilutes, why folds swing ±0.16, and why a 0.027 environment change can swamp a claimed 0.010 effect.

One more thing, from reading your pipeline today

Your run_survival.py builds five feature sets: Celltype (16), PE (16), Raw Coords (4), PE+Celltype (32), PE+Raw (20). The 256-feature neighbour matrix is never used. There are 308 .npy files sitting in data/raw/k10/ — plus k1 through k20 — untouched by the pipeline. That unused folder is worth +0.024 on its own, and it's already computed.

09 What to do next

  1. Shrink the baseline before testing anything else. 272 features against 27 events is why every comparison is mush. Reduce the 256 neighbour features to ~10–20 (PCA, or cluster them). Then a new 12-feature block competes on fair terms instead of being ignored 94% of the time. Nothing else you measure is trustworthy until this is fixed.
  2. Re-run the "does it add?" test properly. With the noise control included every time, as a permanent row. If a block can't beat 12 columns of random numbers, it isn't a result.
  3. Rebuild the graph: Delaunay + a 50 µm cap. Parameter-free, so no per-sample-adaptive k. Euler's formula pins mean degree near 6 for any flat triangulation regardless of density — the density channel is closed by topology, not by hope. And it reaches the ~69 µm scale where biology actually happens.
  4. Push the two-view design, since it's the only thing that escaped. It's your weighting instinct applied where it survives: geometry decides who is adjacent, biology decides what they are. Cheap, untrained, and already proven non-density-bound.
And the honest framing for the thesis

The negative result is the contribution. "In a 2-D geometric graph, degree and density are the same variable, so no purely structural encoding can escape density" — that's a proof, it's supported by three independent measurements, and it explains why an entire family of methods cannot work on this data. That is worth more than a positive result you can't explain. The two-view escape is then the constructive half: here is what to do instead, and here is the evidence it breaks the link.
